Matrix Decompositions

Trace of a matrix

It’s the sum of diagonal elements of a matrix and is the only function satisfying these 4 properties :

  • tr(A+B)=tr(A)+tr(B)\text{tr}(\bold{A+B}) = \text{tr}(\bold{A}) + \text{tr}(\bold{B})tr(A+B)=tr(A)+tr(B)﻿
  • tr(aA)=a tr(A)\text{tr}(a\bold{A}) = a\,\text{tr}(\bold{A})tr(aA)=atr(A)﻿
  • tr(In)=n\text{tr}(\bold{I}_n)=ntr(In​)=n﻿
  • tr(AB)=tr(BA)\text{tr}(\bold{AB}) = \text{tr}(\bold{BA})tr(AB)=tr(BA)﻿

Using the last property, we can get tr(R−1AR)=tr(A)\text{tr}(\bold{R^{-1}AR}) = \text{tr}(\bold{A})tr(R−1AR)=tr(A)﻿ , which means that similar matrices have the same trace, and thus for any linear map f:V→Vf:V\to Vf:V→V﻿, no matter in what bases it’s expressed, as long as it has same bases on either side, (before the map and after the map), we get the same trace for the matrix representing the map.

Eigenspace and geometric multiplicity of eigenvalues

For a matrix A\bold{A}A﻿ , the Eigenspace of the eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ﻿ , denoted by EλE_{\lambda}Eλ​﻿ is the null space of A−λI\bold{A-\lambda I}A−λI﻿ .

The geometric multiplicity is the dimension of this null space and the algebraic multiplicity is the number of times this eigenvalue repeats in the roots of characteristic polynomial.

Eigenvalue properties

  • A matrix A\bold{A}A﻿ and its transpose AT\bold{A}^TAT﻿ have the same eigenvalues (not the same eighenvectors) .
  • Any linear map always has the same eigevalues, no matter how it is expressed , because the diagonal matrix in the decomposition stays the same (upto rearrangement)
  • The sum of eigenvalues is just the trace and the product is the determinant (due to the characteristic polynomial) .
  • Eigenvectors with different eigenvalues are linearly independent. Positive definite matrices

Any matrix A\bold{A}A﻿ such that xTAx>0    ∀  x∈V−{0}\bold{x^TAx}>0\;\; \forall \;\bold{x}\in V-\{0\}xTAx>0∀x∈V−{0}﻿ where VVV﻿ is a vector space is positive definite.

Positive definite matrices always have positive eigenvalues if they are real, since for any such matrix A\bold{A}A﻿ with any eigenvector v\bold{v}v﻿ with eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ﻿ , we have vTAv>0  ⟹  vTλv=λ∣∣v∣∣>0  ⟹  λ>0\bold{v^TAv} >0 \implies \bold{v^T\lambda v} = \lambda ||\bold{v}|| >0 \implies \lambda > 0vTAv>0⟹vTλv=λ∣∣v∣∣>0⟹λ>0﻿ .

Also, by putting the basis vectors instead of v\bold{v}v﻿ , we get that the diagonal entries must also be positive.

Although positive definite matrices don’t have to be symmetric, a lot of texts put the restriction in the definition itself. And I’m going to do the same. So when I say “positive definite”, I actually mean “symmetric positive definite”. Am I being a little bitch here ? Absolutely ! But you’ll just have to deal with it :)

Symmetric matrices

Any matrix which is equal to its transpose is symmetric.

Spectral theorem : if A\bold{A}A﻿ is a symmetric matrix, then the eigenvectors can be orthonormal and span the whole column space of A\bold{A}A﻿ .

Proof of orthogonality : Suppose v1,v2\bold{v_1,v_2} v1​,v2​﻿ are eigenvectotrs with unequal eigenvalues λ1,λ2\lambda_1,\lambda_2λ1​,λ2​﻿ , then v1TAT=(Av1)T=λ1v1T  ⟹  v1TATv2=λ1v1Tv2\bold{v_1^TA^T}=(\bold{Av_1})^T = \lambda_1\bold{v_1}^T \implies \bold{v_1^TA^Tv_2} = \lambda_1\bold{v_1^Tv_2}v1T​AT=(Av1​)T=λ1​v1​T⟹v1T​ATv2​=λ1​v1T​v2​﻿ . But AT=A\bold{A^T=A}AT=A﻿ . Thus, λ1v1Tv2=v1TAv2=v1T(λ2v2)\bold{\lambda_1 v_1^Tv_2 = v_1^TAv_2 =v_1^T(\lambda_2v_2)}λ1​v1T​v2​=v1T​Av2​=v1T​(λ2​v2​)﻿ . But since λ1≠λ2\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2λ1​=λ2​﻿ , so we have v1Tv2=0\bold{v_1^Tv_2=0}v1T​v2​=0﻿ and thus v1,v2\bold{v_1,v_2}v1​,v2​﻿ are perpendicular if they have unequal eigenvalues. In other words, any two eigenvectors from eigenspaces of different eigenvalues are orthogonal. And we already know, that the eigenvectors in the same eigenspace can be chosen such that they are orthogonal. Thus we can create an orthonormal basis using all the eigenvectors.

The other fact that these eigenvectors span the column space is hard to prove. If A\bold{A}A﻿ is not symmetric , then R\bold{R}R﻿ is not an orthogonal matrix, but still has the linearly independent eigenvectors of A\bold{A}A﻿ as its column vectors, and the equation is still correct. This is called eigenvalue decomposition.
