Walk

It is a sequence of adjacent vertices and edges.

Since there can be multiple edges between 2 vertices, just specifying the vertices isn’t enough. Also, just specifying the edge isn’t enough as it doesn’t specify the direction. So we write a walk as something like v1e1v2e2…vnenvn+1v_1e_1v_2e_2\dots v_ne_nv_{n+1}v1​e1​v2​e2​…vn​en​vn+1​﻿ .

Length of a walk

Number of edges in the walk

Closed walk

If the starting and ending vertex are the same, the walk is closed.

Trivial Walk

A walk with 0 length.

Trail

A walk with no repeated edges

Path

A walk with no repeated vertices (and thus also no repeated edges)

Circuit

A closed trail.

Trivial Circuit

A circuit with single vertex and no edges

Eulerian circuit

A circuit that uses all the edges in the graph.

Eulerian Trail

A Eulerian trail is a trail that uses every edge.

Any vertex in the middle of the trail will have an even degree since every time the trail crosses the vertex, we encounter 2 new edges incident on that vertex, and we can only cross the vertex, neither start, nor stop on it.

If the starting and ending vertices are not the same, then these vertices have odd degree since the first and last edge add 1 to the degree of start and end vertex and every crossing adds a 2. If these are the same, then the degree is even. This informal proof isn’t sufficient, and we’ll present a better one soon.

Cycle

A circuit with no repeated vertices, except the first and last.

In a directed graph, the in degree of a vertex is the number of edges incident “to” the vertex. Similarly out degree is number of edges incident “from” the vertex.

Weighted graph

A weighted graph is a graph G = (V, E) along with a function w : E → R that associates a numerical weight to each edge.

The total weight of the graph is the sum of weights of all edges.

A spanning tree of a weighted graph that has the smallest total weight.
